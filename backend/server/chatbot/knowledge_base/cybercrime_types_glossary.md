# Cybercrime Types Glossary

Reference definitions for common cybercrime/fraud terms a victim or reporting
officer might encounter. Use this to explain unfamiliar terms, not as scam-
specific advice (see the other knowledge base files for that).

## Business E-Mail Compromise (BEC)

A scam targeting businesses that regularly do international wire transfers.
Fraudsters compromise or spoof a legitimate business e-mail account (via
social engineering or intrusion) to redirect a real payment to an account they
control. Email Account Compromise (EAC) is the same tactic aimed at
individuals making online transactions with foreign entities.

## Ransomware

Malware that encrypts a victim's files or locks their device, demanding
payment (often in cryptocurrency) for the decryption key. Unlike malware that
quietly steals data, ransomware is overtly disruptive and targets everything
from individual devices to large organizations.

## Email spoofing

Forging an e-mail header so a message appears to come from someone other than
the real sender. Used heavily in phishing because people are more likely to
open and trust mail that looks like it's from someone they know.

## Phishing / Vishing / Smishing

- **Phishing** — fraudulent e-mail impersonating a legitimate source (bank,
  recruiter, company) to extract sensitive information.
- **Vishing** — the same approach by phone call.
- **Smishing** — the same approach by SMS, usually with a link or callback
  number.

## Spear phishing

A targeted form of phishing aimed at specific individuals (often in a
company), using personal details — real name, rank, designation — to make the
message convincing. Spear-phishing e-mails often carry malicious attachments
(XLS/PDF/DOC) that connect to a remote command-and-control server once
opened, and frequently drop a harmless-looking decoy document to mask the
attack.

## Illegal online transactions

Covers fraudulent/unauthorized transactions, claims of lost or stolen
merchandise, and false requests for a refund or return — generally any scheme
where a fraudster poses as a legitimate party to extract money or sensitive
card/account details online.

## Job fraud

Deceiving job seekers with false promises of high salary or easy income to
extract money (fees, deposits) or personal information.

## Cyber defamation

Publishing or transmitting, in electronic form, any statement or image
intended to harm a person's or organization's reputation — the digital
equivalent of traditional defamation.

## Ponzi scheme

An investment scam that pays early "returns" using new investors' money
rather than real profit, collapsing once new investment dries up. Promises of
high returns with little risk are the hallmark warning sign.

## Cyber stalking / cyber bullying

Cyber stalking is persistent harassment of a victim using electronic
communication (e-mail, messaging, social posts). Cyber bullying is repeated,
deliberate harmful or humiliating content about a specific person, spread via
SMS, social media, or other digital channels.

## Cyber pornography

Using cyberspace to create, view, distribute, or publish pornography or
obscene material; a distinct legal offense from other fraud types but often
encountered alongside blackmail/extortion scams (e.g. sextortion).

## Hacking, virus, worm, trojan

- **Hacking** — exploiting weaknesses to gain unauthorized access to a
  computer system or network.
- **Virus** — code that attaches to another program/file and activates when
  that file runs, damaging or degrading the system.
- **Worm** — self-replicating malicious software that spreads across
  connected devices without needing a host file.
- **Trojan** — malware disguised as legitimate software (e.g. a fake office
  app or game) that carries a hidden malicious purpose.

## Website defacement / web jacking

Website defacement is altering a site's visible content (often with hostile
or obscene material) to embarrass its owner or make a political statement.
Web jacking is a phishing variant where attackers clone a real website and
send victims a malicious link to the fake version to harvest credentials or
card details.

## Salami attack

A scheme that makes many small, individually unnoticeable alterations to a
system or account (e.g. fractions of a rupee skimmed from many transactions)
that add up to significant financial gain for the attacker.

## DoS / DDoS attacks

A Denial-of-Service (DoS) attack overwhelms a website or server with traffic
so legitimate users can't access it; a Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS)
attack does the same using many systems at once, making it harder to block.

## Card fraud categories

- **Lost/stolen card fraud** — a lost or stolen card is used by whoever finds
  or takes it.
- **Counterfeit/skimming fraud** — card data is copied (skimmed) onto a
  duplicate card without the owner's knowledge.
- **Card-not-received fraud** — a newly issued card is intercepted in transit
  (e.g. by a courier accomplice or using fake ID) before reaching the real
  owner.
- **Set-up company fraud** — fraudsters rent an address purely to apply for
  cards across multiple banks, collect them, and disappear.
