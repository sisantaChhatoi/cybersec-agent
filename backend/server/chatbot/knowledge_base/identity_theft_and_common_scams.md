# Identity Theft

Identity theft is the act of wrongfully obtaining someone's personal information
(name, phone number, address, bank account number, Aadhaar number, credit/debit
card number) without their permission. Stolen identity information can be used to
access bank accounts, apply for loans or credit cards, file a fraudulent tax
refund, obtain a driver's license or passport in the victim's name, get medical
treatment on the victim's health insurance, or impersonate the victim on social
media and even to the police during an arrest.

## Hacking or gaining access to social media accounts

An attacker who hacks or otherwise gains access to a victim's social media
account can misuse personal information and photographs, post offensive or
morphed content on the victim's profile, or use it to defame the victim — for
example, leaking confidential documents under the victim's name or posting
manipulated obscene images. A common entry point is leaving an account logged
in on a shared computer, such as at a cybercafe.

**Safety tips:** always log out of accounts on shared computers rather than just
closing the browser window; use two-factor authentication (OTP) when using
someone else's computer; never save your username/password in a shared
browser; register your mobile number with social platforms to get alerts on
unauthorized access; permanently delete any documents you downloaded at a
cybercafe before leaving.

## Misuse of photocopies of identity proofs

Sharing photocopies of identity documents (PAN card, Aadhaar, IT returns) with
an untrusted agency — for example, a non-reputed loan agent offering unusually
low interest rates — can let a fraudster apply for multiple loans in the
victim's name across several banks, by altering the photograph, signature,
address, or phone number on the copy while keeping the real document number.

**Safety tips:** never give copies of identity proofs to unknown people or
organizations; be cautious about which proofs you submit and where; strike
through a photocopy and write the specific purpose of its use directly on it so
it can't be reused for another purpose; don't share sensitive personal details
(date of birth, address, family details) on public platforms.

## Credit/debit card skimming

A skimmer is a small device that secretly copies the data on a card's magnetic
stripe — name, card number, expiry date — when the card is swiped, commonly at
restaurants, petrol pumps, or ATMs. The captured data is used to clone the card
and withdraw money or make online transactions. PIN theft often accompanies
skimming: someone nearby (e.g. a waiter handling the card) watches and notes
the PIN as it's entered.

**Safety tips:** keep your card in sight during any swipe transaction — don't
let staff carry it out of view; be alert for card readers that protrude
oddly from a payment machine, a sign of a skimmer; never share your PIN with
anyone, however trusted; cover the keypad with your other hand while entering
a PIN; check ATMs for signs of tampering (new scratches, loose parts, hidden
cameras) before use.

# Psychological Tricks (Phishing, Vishing, Smishing)

Psychological tricks exploit urgency, fear, or excitement to get a victim to
hand over money or sensitive information, typically via fake e-mails, calls, or
SMS.

- **Phishing** — fraudulent e-mail appearing to be from a legitimate source
  (a bank, recruiter, credit card company) aiming to extract sensitive
  information.
- **Vishing** — the same tactic over a phone call instead of e-mail.
- **Smishing** — the same tactic via SMS, usually pushing the recipient to a
  fraudulent website or phone number.

## Lottery fraud

The victim is told (by e-mail/call/SMS) that they've won a lottery, and is
asked to pay a "token amount" or share personal/bank details to release the
winnings. No money is ever released; the victim only loses the amount sent.

**Safety tips:** you cannot win a lottery you never entered; never respond to
unsolicited messages asking for personal or financial details, even if they
promise a credit to your account; verify any government-linked claim against
an official ".gov.in"/".nic.in" domain, not the link in the message; enable
spam filters on your e-mail.

## Credit/debit card fraud (vishing)

A caller claims to be from the victim's bank, says the card has been "blocked
due to suspicious activity," and asks for the card number and PIN to
"re-activate" it. Banks already have these details and never need to ask for
them — the caller may sound convincing by citing a few real details (name,
city, that you hold an account there) to build false trust.

**Safety tips:** a real bank will never call to say your card is blocked and
ask you to "verify" it by giving the card number, PIN, CVV, or OTP; if you're
worried about your card, hang up and call the number printed on the back of
the card yourself.

## Job-related fraud

A fake e-mail offers an attractive job with a high salary, asks for personal
details, and sometimes asks the victim to travel to a specific location/hotel
for an "interview" — which can escalate into being drugged, robbed, or worse
in some reported cases.

**Safety tips:** apply for jobs only through authentic portals or known
recruiters; check that the sender's e-mail domain matches the company's real
domain; treat spelling/grammar errors in a recruiting e-mail as a red flag;
never pay money or travel to an unfamiliar location as a condition of a job
offer.

# Social Media Frauds

## Sympathy and romance fraud

A fraudster befriends the victim on social media, builds trust or affection
over time through frequent chats and calls, then asks for money — citing a
medical emergency or similar urgent need — or, in romance-fraud cases,
manipulates the victim into an intimate relationship and later blackmails them
with photos/videos.

**Safety tips:** be cautious accepting friend requests from strangers; never
send money or share intimate photos with someone you've only met online;
never get into financial dealings with someone you know only through social
media; if you plan to meet an online friend in person, tell family/friends and
meet in a public place.

## Cyber stalking and cyber bullying

Cyber stalking is harassment carried out through electronic means (e-mail, IM,
social posts), often by someone tracking the victim's real-world movements
through what they post (e.g. travel check-ins). Cyber bullying is repeated,
deliberate harmful content (posts, memes, troll pages) aimed at embarrassing
or humiliating a victim, common among teenagers.

**Safety tips:** restrict your social profile's visibility and avoid publicly
posting real-time location/travel plans; watch for behavioral changes in
children (withdrawal, fear of devices) as a sign of bullying or stalking; don't
delete abusive messages — they help in any later investigation; report
incidents to parents, school authorities, or police rather than staying
silent.

# Mobile Application and Online Banking Frauds

Malicious or infected mobile apps (often Trojan-laced clones of popular apps)
can access messages, OTPs, camera, and contacts, show intrusive ads, or sign
the user up for paid subscriptions without consent.

Weak or reused passwords are a common way online banking and other accounts
get hacked — e.g. a password is overheard or guessed (like a birth date), and
because the same password is reused across accounts, one breach compromises
several accounts at once.

**Safety tips:** install apps only from official stores and check permission
requests are sensible for the app's purpose; keep apps updated; use a unique,
strong password (8+ characters, mixed case/numbers/symbols) for every account;
never share your password with anyone, even someone you trust, since you
can't control who they repeat it to; enable 2-step verification (OTP) wherever
available; set a maximum transaction limit on bank/payment accounts so any
single compromise is capped.

# Virus Attacks on Personal Computers

Viruses commonly spread through infected external storage (USB/pen drives),
downloads from untrusted websites (disguised as music/video files), or
installation of pirated/unofficial software and games.

**Safety tips:** keep an updated antivirus/firewall running; scan external
storage devices before opening files from them; avoid pirated software; check
for "https" in the address bar before any online transaction; only download
software from official, trusted sources.
