# Digital Arrest Scam — Core Pattern

A "digital arrest" scam is a phone or video call in which a fraudster
impersonates a law-enforcement or regulatory authority (CBI, ED, Customs,
TRAI, local police, or a courier company acting on their behalf) and
convinces the victim that they are under investigation or "digital arrest"
and must stay on the call, follow instructions, and pay money to avoid
arrest. Real Indian law-enforcement agencies never conduct arrests, demand
payments, or hold "virtual hearings" over WhatsApp, Skype, or a phone call.
There is no such legal concept as a "digital arrest" in Indian law.

## How the scam typically unfolds

1. **Intro / hook** — The victim gets a call claiming a parcel/SIM/bank
   account in their name is linked to an illegal shipment (drugs, fake
   passports) or financial crime. The caller often spoofs a number to look
   like it's from a local police station or courier company.
2. **Authority handoff** — The call escalates to someone impersonating a CBI,
   ED, or police officer, sometimes in a fake uniform on video, with a
   forged ID card or "warrant" shown on screen.
3. **Threat and urgency** — The victim is told they face immediate arrest,
   asset freeze, or a non-bailable case unless they cooperate right now. The
   pressure is deliberately constant so the victim has no time to think or
   verify independently.
4. **Isolation instructions** — The victim is told not to disconnect the
   call, not to tell family members, and sometimes to stay on camera
   continuously ("digital arrest") while moving to a private room.
5. **Extraction** — The victim is asked to transfer money to "verify
   accounts" or pay a "fine" to avoid arrest, sometimes via multiple small
   transfers to avoid bank fraud triggers, or asked to install remote-access
   apps (AnyDesk, TeamViewer) so the scammer can directly operate the
   victim's banking apps.

## Red flags to listen for

- Caller claims to be CBI, ED, Customs, TRAI, or police and threatens arrest
  over a phone/video call.
- Instructions to not hang up, not tell anyone, or stay on camera
  continuously.
- Any request to install AnyDesk, TeamViewer, or similar remote-access apps.
- Any request for OTPs, Aadhaar number, bank PINs, or to transfer money to
  "verify" an account or avoid a "non-bailable warrant."
- Pressure to act within minutes, with no opportunity to call the agency
  back on an officially listed number.

## What to tell a worried caller

- No real police, CBI, ED, or Customs officer will ever arrest someone over
  a phone or video call, or ask for money/OTP to "avoid arrest."
- They should hang up immediately, not share any OTP, PIN, or install any
  remote-access app, and not transfer any money.
- If money has already been transferred, they should call the **1930**
  cybercrime helpline immediately (the faster a complaint is filed, the
  higher the chance of freezing the transferred funds) and file a report at
  **cybercrime.gov.in**.
- They can independently verify any claimed case by visiting the local
  police station in person or contacting the agency through its official
  website — never through a number or link given by the caller.
