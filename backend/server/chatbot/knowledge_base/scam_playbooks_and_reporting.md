# Reporting, Helpline Facts & Universal Safety Rules

## Official reporting channels (verify against cybercrime.gov.in before relying on these)
- **1930** — national cybercrime helpline. Toll-free, available 24x7, works
  PAN-India with no STD code. Operated by the Indian Cyber Crime Coordination
  Centre (I4C) under the Ministry of Home Affairs. It replaced the older 155260
  number in 2021. Call it especially for financial fraud / money lost.
- **https://cybercrime.gov.in** — National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (NCRP).
  File a written complaint here; you receive an acknowledgement number and can
  attach evidence (screenshots, transaction IDs, a short written account).
- For losses above Rs 50,000 or identity theft, also approach your local cyber
  cell / police station with the portal acknowledgement.

## Why speed matters
Report as fast as possible. Freezing the money trail works far better within the
first 2-4 hours of the fraud. The sooner you report, the better the chance of
freezing the receiving account and recovering funds.

## Beware the "callback" follow-up scam
After reporting, some victims get a second call from a spoofed number claiming to
be 1930 or the bank's fraud cell: "Your case is registered — share the OTP to
process your refund" or "pay a small verification fee." This is a SECOND scam.
1930 will never call you from a 10-digit mobile number, never ask for an OTP, and
never ask for a fee to process a refund. If this happens, hang up and call 1930
yourself.

## Universal red flags across ALL scams
- Any request for an OTP, UPI PIN, full card number, CVV, or net-banking password.
  No legitimate bank, agency, or company ever needs these from you.
- Any demand to install a screen-sharing / remote-access app (AnyDesk, TeamViewer)
  on someone's instruction.
- Pressure and urgency: "act now," "within hours," "or you'll be arrested/blocked."
- A demand to pay money upfront to receive money (refund, prize, job, investment,
  parcel release, or to "prove innocence").
- Secrecy: being told not to tell family or to stay alone on a call.
- Being contacted from a number/link the caller provides and told to use only that.

## What to do if you have ALREADY been scammed
1. Call **1930** immediately — speed is critical.
2. Call your **bank** to block the card / account / report the unauthorized
   transaction and request a freeze on the beneficiary.
3. File a complaint at **https://cybercrime.gov.in**; save the acknowledgement PDF.
4. If you installed a remote-access app, uninstall it, disconnect the internet,
   and consider resetting the device; change banking passwords from a safe device.
5. Keep all evidence: SMS/WhatsApp screenshots, numbers, transaction IDs, the app/
   website used.

## Tone for assisting a worried person
Stay calm, clear, and non-alarmist. Reassure them that hanging up and reporting is
the right move, that no real officer arrests people over video calls, and that
acting quickly improves recovery. Never ask them to share sensitive details (OTP,
full card number, passwords) with you either.


# Digital Arrest Scam

## What it is
A "digital arrest" scam is a fraud where criminals impersonate law-enforcement or
government officers (CBI, ED, Customs, Narcotics Control Bureau, TRAI, police,
Mumbai/Delhi Cyber Cell) over a phone or video call. They falsely accuse the
victim of a serious crime, then keep them on a continuous video call for hours or
days under "digital arrest" — a fake legal status that does not exist in Indian
law — while extracting money. There is NO such thing as a "digital arrest" in
Indian law. No real agency arrests, interrogates, or holds anyone over a video
call.

## How the scam unfolds (typical stages)
1. **Intro:** Caller claims to be an officer of CBI/ED/Customs/police. May cite a
   fake case number, your real name, or partial Aadhaar to sound credible. Often
   begins as an automated call about a "parcel" or "SIM/number misuse."
2. **Threat:** Claims your Aadhaar, bank account, or a parcel is linked to money
   laundering, drugs, or a serious crime. Says a warrant is issued and you face
   immediate arrest.
3. **Isolation:** Orders you to stay on the call, keep your video on, go to a room
   alone, and tell NO ONE — not even family. This isolation is the core of the
   scam; it prevents you from getting a reality check.
4. **Extraction:** Demands you transfer money to a "verification" / "safe" / "RBI"
   account to "prove your innocence," promising a refund. Or demands OTP, Aadhaar,
   bank details, or that you install a screen-sharing app (AnyDesk, TeamViewer).

## Red flags (any ONE means it is almost certainly a scam)
- The words "digital arrest," "you cannot disconnect," "do not tell anyone."
- A demand to keep video on continuously or stay on the call for hours.
- A demand to transfer money to "verify," to an "RBI account," or a "safe account."
- A request to install AnyDesk / TeamViewer / any screen-share app.
- A request for OTP, full Aadhaar number, bank or card details.
- Threats of immediate arrest unless you act right now.
- Pressure to keep it secret from family.
- An "officer" who calls on a normal 10-digit mobile or international number.

## What real agencies actually do
- Real CBI, ED, Customs, police, or any agency NEVER arrest, interrogate, or
  detain anyone over a phone or video call.
- They NEVER ask you to transfer money to prove innocence. No legitimate
  investigation works this way.
- They NEVER ask you to keep a video call running or to hide the matter from
  family.
- Real summons/notices are delivered in writing/in person, and you have the right
  to consult a lawyer and verify at the actual office.
- A genuine investigation lets you contact the agency through official, publicly
  listed numbers — never a number the caller gives you.

## Correct advice
- Hang up. It is not rude to disconnect a scammer impersonating an officer.
- Do not transfer any money, share any OTP/Aadhaar/bank detail, or install any app.
- Do not stay on the call out of fear. No real officer can "arrest" you by phone.
- Tell a family member or friend immediately — secrecy is exactly what the scam
  needs.
- Verify independently: contact the real agency or your local police using their
  official public number, not the caller's number.
- Report it (see reporting steps below). Report fast — freezing fraudulent funds
  works far better within the first 2-4 hours.

## Reporting
- Call the national cybercrime helpline **1930** (toll-free, 24x7, works PAN-India).
- File a written complaint at **https://cybercrime.gov.in** (National Cyber Crime
  Reporting Portal). Use the mobile number involved; attach screenshots and a
  short written account. You receive an acknowledgement number.
- For losses above Rs 50,000 or identity theft, also visit your local cyber cell
  with the portal acknowledgement.

## Note on impersonation of the helpline itself
1930 will NEVER call you back from a 10-digit mobile number asking for an OTP or a
"verification fee" to "process your refund." If a 10-digit number claims to be
1930 or your bank's fraud cell and asks for OTP or payment, it is a follow-up
scam — hang up and call 1930 yourself.

# Courier / Customs Parcel Scam

## What it is
The victim gets a call (often starting as an automated/IVR message) claiming a
parcel in their name has been seized by Customs, FedEx, DHL, BlueDart, or the
police because it contains illegal items — drugs, fake passports, weapons, or
banned goods. The caller then either extorts a "fine/clearance fee" or escalates
into a full digital arrest scam (impersonating Customs or Narcotics officers).

## How the scam unfolds
1. Automated call: "A parcel sent in your name has been seized," press a key to
   "speak to an officer."
2. A fake Customs/FedEx/police officer says the parcel contained illegal items
   (commonly: drugs, MDMA, fake documents) and your Aadhaar is linked to it.
3. Threat of arrest; the call is often transferred to a fake "police" or
   "Narcotics" officer (the handoff is a classic tactic).
4. Demand for a "fine," "clearance fee," or transfer to a "verification account,"
   or a demand for personal/bank details. May escalate to digital arrest.

## Red flags
- An unexpected call about a parcel you did not send.
- Claims the parcel contains drugs/illegal items linked to your Aadhaar.
- Being transferred from "courier" to "Customs" to "police" during one call.
- Any demand for a fee, fine, or money transfer to release the parcel or avoid
  arrest.
- Requests for Aadhaar, OTP, or bank details.

## What real couriers and Customs actually do
- Real courier companies do not phone you to say a parcel contains drugs and that
  you will be arrested. Delivery issues are handled via the company's official app/
  website and tracking number.
- Real Customs does not collect fines over the phone or via UPI/bank transfer to a
  personal "verification account."
- No agency asks for your OTP or full bank details to "clear" a parcel.

## Correct advice
- Hang up. Do not press any key, do not "speak to the officer."
- Do not pay any fee or share any details.
- If you genuinely expect a parcel, check only via the courier's official app or
  website using the real tracking number — never a number or link the caller gives.
- Report via 1930 and https://cybercrime.gov.in if money was lost or details shared.

## Reporting
- Helpline **1930** (24x7). Portal **https://cybercrime.gov.in**.
- Report quickly; early reporting improves the chance of freezing transferred funds.

# KYC Update / Account-Block Scam

## What it is
The victim gets an SMS, WhatsApp, or call claiming their bank account, SIM, PAN,
or a wallet (Paytm/PhonePe) will be "blocked" or "suspended" today unless they
"update KYC" immediately. The goal is to make the victim click a phishing link,
share an OTP, or install a remote-access app, leading to account takeover.

## How the scam unfolds
1. Urgent message: "Your account/SIM/PAN will be blocked today. Update KYC now."
2. A link to a fake bank/wallet page, OR a call from a fake "bank official."
3. Victim is asked to enter card/account details, share an OTP, or install an app
   like AnyDesk so the scammer can "help update KYC."
4. With the OTP / remote access, the scammer drains the account.

## Red flags
- Urgent threat that an account/SIM/PAN will be blocked "today" or "within hours."
- A link to update KYC (real banks do not update KYC via random SMS/WhatsApp links).
- Any request for OTP, full card number, CVV, PIN, or net-banking password.
- A request to install AnyDesk / TeamViewer / "support" apps.

## What real banks actually do
- Banks NEVER ask for OTP, PIN, CVV, or full card number by call, SMS, email, or
  link. Their staff will tell you they will never ask for these.
- Real KYC updates are done in the branch or inside the bank's official app, not
  via a link someone sends you.
- Banks do not ask you to install screen-sharing apps.

## Correct advice
- Do not click the link. Do not share OTP/PIN/CVV/card number with anyone.
- Do not install any app on someone's phone instruction.
- If unsure about your KYC, call the number printed on the back of your debit card
  or visit your branch — never the number in the suspicious message.
- If you shared an OTP or card details, call your bank to block the card
  immediately, then report.

## Reporting
- Helpline **1930** (24x7). Portal **https://cybercrime.gov.in**.
- Report within hours for the best chance of freezing/recovering funds.

# UPI / Refund / Wrong-Payment Scams

## What it is
A family of scams abusing UPI (PhonePe, Google Pay, Paytm, BHIM). The common
thread: the scammer tricks the victim into APPROVING a payment or sharing a UPI
PIN, while making it feel like they are RECEIVING money.

## Common variants
- **"Wrong transfer, please return it":** Scammer says they accidentally sent you
  money and asks you to send it back. (Often no money actually arrived, or it was
  a pending request disguised as a credit.)
- **Collect-request trick:** Scammer sends a UPI "request money" / "collect"
  request. If you approve and enter your PIN, money LEAVES your account. You never
  enter a UPI PIN to RECEIVE money.
- **Fake refund/cashback:** "You are eligible for a refund/cashback — scan this QR
  or approve this request." Scanning a QR or approving a request SENDS money.
- **Marketplace/OLX buyer scam:** A "buyer" insists on paying via a QR code or
  collect request, which actually withdraws money from the seller.

## The single most important UPI rule
You NEVER need to enter your UPI PIN, scan a QR code, or approve a "collect
request" to RECEIVE money. Entering your PIN or scanning a QR always SENDS money
FROM your account. If someone says you must do these to "get" money, it is a scam.

## Red flags
- Being asked to enter your UPI PIN, scan a QR, or approve a request to "receive"
  a refund, prize, or returned payment.
- Urgency and a stranger guiding you step-by-step through your UPI app.
- A "buyer" who only pays via QR/collect request.

## Correct advice
- Do not enter your UPI PIN or scan any QR to receive money. Stop and check your
  actual balance independently.
- If someone claims a wrong transfer, ask them to raise it with their own bank/UPI
  app; do not send money on a stranger's word.
- Never approve a collect request from an unknown source.

## Reporting
- Helpline **1930** (24x7). Portal **https://cybercrime.gov.in**.
- Keep the UPI transaction ID / reference number ready when you report.

# Job, Task, Investment & Lottery Scams

## What they are
Scams that lure victims with the promise of money — a job, easy "task" earnings,
high investment returns, or a lottery win — then extract fees or larger
"investments" that are never returned.

## Task / part-time job scam
- Message on WhatsApp/Telegram offering easy money for "liking videos," "rating
  hotels," or simple tasks.
- Small early payouts build trust, then victims are pushed to deposit money for
  "prepaid tasks" / "merchant tasks" with promised higher returns. The deposits
  are lost.
- Red flags: unsolicited job offer, small initial earnings, then a demand to
  deposit your own money to "unlock" bigger earnings.

## Investment / trading scam ("pig butchering")
- A stranger (often via WhatsApp, a dating app, or a stock-tip group) befriends
  the victim and introduces a "guaranteed high return" trading/crypto app or group.
- A fake app shows growing profits; withdrawals are blocked until more "tax,"
  "fee," or "minimum balance" is paid.
- Red flags: guaranteed/very high returns, a slick app you were referred to
  privately, withdrawals blocked behind extra fees, pressure to invest more.

## Lottery / prize / lucky-draw scam
- Call/SMS: "You won Rs X lakh/crore in a KBC/lucky draw — pay a processing fee/
  GST/tax to release the prize."
- Each fee is followed by another. No real prize exists.
- Red flags: you won a lottery you never entered; any demand to pay a fee to
  receive a prize.

## Universal rule for all of these
A legitimate job, investment, or prize NEVER requires you to pay money upfront to
receive money. Any "pay first to earn/withdraw/claim" demand is a scam. Guaranteed
high returns do not exist — that promise itself is the warning sign.

## Correct advice
- Do not pay any upfront fee, deposit, tax, or "processing charge."
- Do not move money into an app or account a stranger referred you to.
- Verify a company/recruiter through official, independent channels; verify
  investment platforms with SEBI-registered status.
- Be wary of guaranteed returns and of online-only "friends" steering you to invest.

## Reporting
- Helpline **1930** (24x7). Portal **https://cybercrime.gov.in**.
- Report early; keep transaction IDs, chat screenshots, and the app/website details.
